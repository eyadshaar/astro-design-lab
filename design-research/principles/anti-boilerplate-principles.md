# Anti-Boilerplate Design Principles
## How to Build Websites That Do Not Look AI-Generated

AI-generated websites fail because they rely on shallow, repeatable patterns that require no design judgment. Every principle below forces genuine design decisions — not defaults.

---

## IDENTITY & METAPHOR

**1. Choose one dominant visual metaphor for the entire site.**
Generic AI sites have no metaphor. They use "clean design" as a shield for "no idea." Every memorable site has an underlying concept — a map, a command console, a magazine spread, a kitchen, a control room, a workshop bench. Pick one and commit to it.

**2. The metaphor must connect to the business model, not just the industry.**
A bakery metaphor for a bakery is obvious. A bakery metaphor for a B2B analytics tool is interesting. Find the non-obvious connection.

**3. Every section should express the same metaphor differently.**
The hero might show the metaphor in full; the features section breaks it into parts; the CTA frames it as the user's to claim. Coherence through repetition with variation.

**4. Name the visual device explicitly in your spec.**
"Command console hero" is a visual device. "Clean modern navbar" is not. Name the specific thing.

**5. The unexpected visual device should appear in at least one section, deliberately.**
Timeline. Receipt. Map. Control panel. Blueprint. Itinerary. Ledger. Field journal. One per site minimum — placed where it most naturally fits.

---

## LAYOUT & RHYTHM

**6. Do not use a centered hero unless deliberately justified.**
The centered hero is the single most overused layout in AI web design. Alternatives: asymmetric split, full-bleed image left with text right, top-edge-to-edge cinematic, left-weighted editorial, right-heavy data-first.

**7. Alternate section density deliberately.**
Quiet section → dense proof section → spacious emotional section → structured data section. AI sites repeat the same vertical rhythm everywhere. Humans vary it.

**8. Avoid identical card designs across sections.**
If hero has a card, features has a card, testimonials has a card, and pricing has a card — that's four identical card components in four different sections. Vary the card format: some horizontal, some vertical, some edge-to-edge, some small.

**9. Use grid breaks intentionally.**
Break the grid to create emphasis, not just because CSS Grid makes it easy. A full-bleed image section followed by a narrow column section creates drama; a sequence of identical columns creates monotony.

**10. Mobile is its own designed experience, not stacked desktop sections.**
Design the mobile layout first for content-heavy sites, or design it alongside desktop. Never assume "it stacks well."

**11. Use asymmetric compositions over centered symmetry.**
Centered symmetry is safe. Asymmetry creates visual tension and personality. Use it.

---

## TYPOGRAPHY

**12. Pick a specific typeface, not a Google Fonts default.**
The default choices (Inter, Roboto, Poppins, Lato) are default exactly because everyone uses them. Find a typeface with character that matches the brand personality. Editorial sites: something with ink traps or high contrast. Technical sites: something with clear numerals. Luxury: refined serif.

**13. Use a typographic scale that creates hierarchy, not just size.**
6 levels minimum. The differences between sizes should be meaningful, not "this is 16px and this is 18px." Larger contrast is more readable.

**14. One section should be intentionally type-forward.**
One section where typography IS the visual — large type, overlapping words, expressive letter spacing. This creates a memorable moment.

**15. Avoid generic heading capitalization.**
"Welcome To Our Platform" looks like template text. "Welcome to our platform" with intentional sizing is more interesting.

---

## COLOR

**16. Reject the purple-to-blue gradient as hero background.**
This is the single most identifiable marker of AI-generated design. If you use a gradient, make it surprising: warm amber to deep rust, forest green to slate, burgundy to black.

**17. Use color to signal brand personality, not just brand recognition.**
Blue is not "trustworthy" by default — it's been overused until it signals "generic SaaS." Find a color territory that your competitors have not claimed.

**18. At least one section should break the color system deliberately.**
A full-black section in an otherwise light palette. A saturated accent color used as a background. This creates visual drama.

**19. Use a color for meaning, not just decoration.**
The CTA button is always the same color because it always means "action." The danger/warning color appears only in specific contexts. Consistency in meaning is clarity.

**20. Dark mode should be a different experience, not just inverted.**
If you offer dark mode, redesign it — don't just flip the CSS variables. Shadows, depth, and contrast behave differently in dark environments.

---

## NAVIGATION

**21. Navigation should communicate product complexity level.**
A 3-page brochure site should have a simple top bar. A complex B2B product needs mega menus, search, and documentation links. Mismatching complexity to navigation structure is confusing.

**22. One navbar element should be unexpected.**
A command palette. A language selector with flag icons. A persistent status indicator. One thing that makes returning visitors remember the nav.

**23. The CTA button belongs in the navbar if conversion is the primary goal.**
For landing pages, SaaS demos, lead gen — the CTA should be immediately visible, not buried below the fold. For editorial or portfolio sites, it can be in the final CTA only.

---

## CTA & CONVERSION

**24. Never write "Get Started" as a CTA button.**
"Get Started" tells the user nothing. "Book a 30-minute audit," "See pricing," "Start my free trial," "Send project brief" — these name the action AND the outcome.

**25. CTAs must be surrounded by proof.**
The CTA button is the climax of a proof sequence. Show the outcome first. Show evidence second. Put the button third. Never ask for action before establishing credibility.

**26. Each page should have exactly one primary CTA.**
Multiple CTAs divide attention. The navbar has one. The hero has one. The final section has one. Secondary actions are smaller and fewer.

**27. Ask for the smallest possible commitment first.**
"Start free trial" is smaller than "Talk to sales." "Get the free guide" is smaller than "Buy now." Reduce friction at the top of the funnel.

**28. Use copy that names the user's desired outcome.**
"Build better websites" is vague. "Ship your landing page by Friday" is specific and outcome-oriented.

---

## CONTENT & COPY

**29. Write copy for a specific person, not everyone.**
"The best project management tool for teams" speaks to no one. "For engineering teams shipping fast and drowning in status meetings" speaks to someone specific. Specificity is credibility.

**30. Replace generic feature bullets with use-case narratives.**
Don't list "real-time collaboration." Show a screenshot of two team members editing the same document with their cursors visible. Show the moment, not the feature.

**31. Use realistic data, not fake metrics.**
"2.5x more productive" means nothing. "Our users save 3 hours per week on status meetings" is believable. Use real numbers with real context.

**32. Avoid AI buzzwords: "supercharge," "revolutionize," "transform," "leverage."**
These words appear in every generic AI-generated site. They mean nothing. Replace them with specific claims.

**33. Write one section in the user's words, not the company's.**
The FAQ section should use questions a real user would ask, phrased the way they think. Not "How can our platform help your organization optimize synergies?" but "Why is this better than the spreadsheet I already have?"

---

## ANIMATION & MOTION

**34. Motion must clarify hierarchy, not decorate.**
Animation that draws attention to nothing important is noise. Every animation should guide the eye to the next piece of information or confirm an interaction.

**35. Scroll animations should feel like reveals, not performances.**
Text that types itself out. Images that slide in from a direction that matches reading flow. These feel like natural discovery. Page-load animations that hold the user hostage feel like advertising.

**36. Use reduced-motion as the default consideration.**
Always build for no-animation first. Add animation as enhancement, not requirement.

**37. One motion signature per site.**
One recurring animation motif that appears across sections — a parallax depth effect, a text mask reveal, a horizontal scroll gallery. Repetition of one technique creates coherence.

---

## SECTIONS

**38. Replace "three feature cards" with a use-case journey.**
Three cards side by side is the default AI output. Instead: show one feature in depth with screenshot, annotation, and context. Or show a sequence of steps the user takes, with the features embedded in the steps.

**39. Use a comparison table instead of a features checklist.**
Comparison tables are scannable and honest. Feature checklists imply "we have everything." Comparison against alternatives (including "do nothing") is more credible.

**40. Show the tool in action, not a screenshot of the tool.**
A GIF or video of the actual interface doing something real is more convincing than a hero screenshot. Static product images are the default AI approach — go one step further.

**41. Testimonials need attribution, specificity, and photo.**
"A great tool!" — no attribution, no photo, no specificity. "Since we started using this, our incident response time dropped from 4 hours to 45 minutes. The on-call rotation actually sleeps now." — specific, attributed, believable.

---

## IMAGES & MEDIA

**42. No stock photos of laptops with code, floating devices, or 'team high-fiving.'**
These are the universal signifiers of "we hired an AI to write this." Use real photography, abstract visuals, custom illustration, or no images — but not stock tech clichés.

**43. Use one image type consistently per section type.**
Hero: one dominant image or video. Features: annotated screenshots or diagrams. Social proof: real human faces. Use image type intentionally.

**44. Abstract and geometric visuals often outperform photography for B2B/technical.**
An annotated diagram of a workflow is often more credible than a stock photo. A geometric pattern related to the industry is more memorable than generic office photography.

---

## STRUCTURE & INFORMATION ARCHITECTURE

**45. Lead with the outcome, not the company story.**
"Founded in 2018 with a vision to..." is a company-centric opener. "Our customers ship 40% faster" is user-centric. Start with what the user gets, not who built it.

**46. Information hierarchy should match conversion priority.**
What do you want users to notice first? Second? Third? Build that hierarchy explicitly. Do not let the design default (biggest element = most important) determine priority.

**47. Use the F-pattern for content-heavy pages.**
Users scan in an F-pattern. Put the most important information top-left, next most important top-right, third tier below the fold. Structure content to match how people read.

---

## AVOIDING GENERIC AI PATTERNS

**48. Identify the generic AI pattern that would have been used, then deliberately deviate.**
Every time you spec a section, ask: "What would an AI generate here by default?" Then do the opposite of that. This single question is the most powerful anti-boilerplate tool.

**49. The bar for "good enough" must be higher than "it works."**
Generic AI sites work technically. They do not feel intentional. Every element on the page should feel like it was placed by someone with a reason.

**50. Every element must be answerable to the question: "Why is this here?"**
If you cannot answer that question with a specific reason, remove the element. Design is subtraction as much as addition.
